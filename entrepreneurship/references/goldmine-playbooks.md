# Goldmine Playbooks

Use this file when the user explicitly wants more specificity or the decision maps closely to the founder interviews about pricing, positioning, open-source monetization, content, SEO, transparency, free tools, complexity, solo-founder operations, or audience-to-product fit.

When using this file:
- name the founder move if it clearly fits
- say what made it work in that case
- say what would make it fail here
- prefer one named pattern over a vague blend of five
- say exactly what to copy and exactly what not to copy

## 1. Adam Wathan: Put Distribution And Taste Behind The Same Offer

Use when:
- the founder already has an audience, reputation, or taste moat
- the user is choosing between a business that compounds current distribution and one that starts a second treadmill

Default:
- prefer products, education, templates, or media that reinforce the same audience and taste loop

What this means in practice:
- The best business is often not the one with the biggest TAM. It is the one where the founder's existing audience, credibility, and sense of taste directly improve the offer.
- If the founder is known for a certain craft, content, or community, use that to shorten trust-building and distribution.
- When product, audience, and creative taste all reinforce each other, the business becomes easier to market and harder to imitate.

Failure mode:
- choosing a business that looks lucrative on paper but forces the founder onto an unrelated distribution treadmill

Fastest next move:
- list the audiences the founder can already reach credibly
- list which product ideas get stronger because that audience already trusts the founder's judgment
- reject ideas that require building an unrelated audience from scratch

Source:
- `76 Distribution & Taste w Adam Wathan.txt`

## 2. Adam Wathan: Separate "I Want To Build This" From "I Want To Operate This"

Use when:
- the user is attracted to a business because it seems prestigious, technically interesting, or admired by peers
- the decision is really about whether to enter a category with ongoing complexity and operational drag

Default:
- choose the business you are willing to run every week, not just the one you are excited to launch

What this means in practice:
- A lot of founder regret comes from confusing launch excitement with long-term operator fit.
- Complexity is not a one-time build tax. It becomes a permanent support, maintenance, and attention tax.
- If the business model makes the founder's day-to-day worse, the fact that it is admired by smart people does not save it.

Failure mode:
- signing up for a business whose recurring operational burden the founder quietly hates

Fastest next move:
- write down the weekly operating reality of the business after launch: support, sales, bugs, compliance, hiring, and maintenance
- compare that to the founder's actual preferences, not their aspirational identity

Sources:
- `76 Distribution & Taste w Adam Wathan.txt`
- `Resisting Complexity - Adam Wathan - Laracon US 2018.txt`

## 3. John O'Nolan: Raise Prices To Improve Customer Quality, Not Just Revenue

Use when:
- the user suspects they are underpriced
- low-priced customers are support-heavy, impatient, or low-commitment
- the question is whether a price increase is worth the conversion hit

Default:
- test a meaningful price increase because cheaper customers are often worse customers

What this means in practice:
- Price is partly psychological and shapes customer behavior, not just top-line revenue.
- Higher prices can produce more patient customers, better trust, and lower support pain even if conversions drop.
- If doubling price cuts conversion less than half, the business can improve on both economics and support quality.
- Test higher pricing on new customers first when trust with the current base matters.

Failure mode:
- keeping prices low to preserve vanity conversion while attracting the worst-fit customers

Fastest next move:
- measure support load and retention by plan
- run a higher-price test on new customers
- compare revenue per visitor, support burden, and customer quality instead of conversion rate alone

Sources:
- `Pricing SaaS Products with John O'Nolan from Ghost (Yo! Podcast 002).txt`
- `Interview John O'Nolan, Co-Founder & CEO of Ghost, on staying true to your values, pricing, & more.txt`

## 4. John O'Nolan: Translate Technical Advantages Into Buyer Language

Use when:
- the product has a technical differentiator that users do not naturally understand
- the user wants to position open source, architecture, infrastructure, or another technical choice

Default:
- market the buyer outcome, not the technical mechanism

What this means in practice:
- "Open source" is often too technical and too abstract for many buyers.
- The better story might be independence, control, no rug pull risk, portability, or long-term safety.
- The right message depends on the audience. Developers may want the technical story directly; non-developers usually want the consequence of that story.
- A good position names the disease before offering the cure. Independence resonates most when the buyer has felt platform risk before.

Failure mode:
- leading with technical identity language that the buyer does not understand or care about

Fastest next move:
- name the actual buyer fear the technical choice prevents
- rewrite the positioning around that fear and outcome
- keep the technical explanation one level deeper for the audience that wants it

- `Interview John O'Nolan, Co-Founder & CEO of Ghost, on staying true to your values, pricing, & more.txt`

## 5. John O'Nolan: Monetize Open Source By Selling Convenience, Reliability, And Aggregated Scale

Use when:
- the user is building around open source
- the question is how to make money without betraying the project's values

Default:
- keep the product open, then charge for the easiest, safest, best-configured way to run it

What this means in practice:
- Hosted convenience can be valuable even when self-hosting is possible.
- Buyers are not just paying for servers. They are paying to avoid setup, maintenance, updates, deliverability, security work, and operational babysitting.
- Scale on the vendor side creates real economic advantages such as better pricing with providers and better defaults across the stack.
- This works best when the hosted offer is genuinely easier and better than the average self-hosted setup.

Failure mode:
- assuming self-hosting is the real competitor when the true competitor is "I do not want this operational burden at all"

Fastest next move:
- list the buyer's hidden operational costs if they self-host
- package the hosted offer around convenience, reliability, and preconfigured integrations
- check that the hosted version is clearly the easiest path for the intended customer

Sources:
- `Non-profit, open source and making $7m ARR - John O'Nolan from Ghost.txt`
- `Interview John O'Nolan, Co-Founder & CEO of Ghost, on staying true to your values, pricing, & more.txt`

## 6. Josh Pigford: Build Tentpole Launches Backwards From A Narrative, Then "Clean The Room" First

Use when:
- the team is planning a big multi-quarter feature or repositioning
- the user is asking how to manage a large launch without losing the thread

Default:
- start from the narrative of the major feature, then decompose the dependency tree and ship preparatory work that makes the launch possible

What this means in practice:
- A tentpole launch is not one ticket. It is a story with prerequisite work underneath it.
- The preparatory work often includes redesigning adjacent surfaces so the big feature has somewhere clean to land.
- The roadmap should explain why the big thing matters first, then break it into sub-capabilities and enabling changes.
- Good sequencing finds places where prerequisite work also improves the existing product so the team gets value before the headline feature ships.

Failure mode:
- treating a major launch as one giant blob and missing the hidden interface, onboarding, and workflow changes it depends on

Fastest next move:
- write the one-sentence narrative for the tentpole
- draw the dependency tree underneath it
- mark which subprojects both unblock the tentpole and improve the current product today

Source:
- `Managing tentpole launches at Gumroad ft. Josh Pigford from Baremetrics.txt`

## 7. Josh Pigford: Use Transparency Only When It Tells A Buyer-Relevant Story

Use when:
- the founder is considering public revenue, open startup metrics, or build-in-public transparency
- the user assumes transparency is automatically good marketing

Default:
- be transparent only when the thing being shared proves something the buyer already cares about

What this means in practice:
- Transparency for its own sake is mostly performance art.
- It works when the audience is similar to the customer, so the story doubles as proof and trust.
- For Baremetrics, transparency fit because the customers were other SaaS businesses who cared about the same numbers.
- Public sharing can create copycats, so the upside must be real and audience-specific.

Failure mode:
- oversharing because the startup world applauds it, even though the buyers do not care

Fastest next move:
- write the exact buyer belief the transparency is supposed to strengthen
- if you cannot name it clearly, do not publish it
- if you can, share the minimum proof that makes that story more believable

Sources:
- `How Baremetrics Used Transparency to Grow to $1.5M ARR.txt`
- `Josh Pigford, Founder of Baremetrics.txt`

## 8. Pieter Levels: Build A Product Flywheel Around One Worldview And One Audience

Use when:
- the founder has multiple products and worries they look scattered
- the user has a clear worldview, lifestyle, or customer identity that could unify several offers

Default:
- let multiple products exist only if they reinforce the same audience, worldview, and distribution loop

What this means in practice:
- A portfolio can still be one business if the products all orbit the same identity and audience.
- Content, product experiments, and revenue lines can feed the same flywheel when each new project deepens authority with the same people.
- The point is not to be random and prolific. The point is to keep returning to the same user, the same worldview, and the same pains.

Failure mode:
- launching unrelated products that split attention and build no cumulative distribution advantage

Fastest next move:
- describe the audience and worldview that tie the current projects together
- kill or pause anything that does not strengthen that shared loop
- bias the next product toward the same people, not just the highest standalone revenue idea

Sources:
- `How to get rich as a solo programmer  Pieter Levels and Lex Fridman.txt`
- `How Pieter Levels Makes $2.7MYear With 0 Employees.txt`

## 9. Pieter Levels: Optimize For Feedback-Loop Speed And Relentless Simplicity

Use when:
- the founder is overengineering
- the user is defending a complex stack before they have tight customer feedback
- the team needs to move faster with fewer people

Default:
- choose the stack and workflow that let you ship customer-visible changes fastest

What this means in practice:
- The right stack for a small software business is often the one the founder can move in quickly, not the one peers respect most.
- Faster deploy cycles create faster customer feedback, which compounds into better product decisions.
- Customers feel heard when they see ideas become features quickly.
- Radical simplicity also makes heavy automation and lean operations possible.

Failure mode:
- choosing infrastructure prestige over learning speed, then slowing the whole business down

Fastest next move:
- measure how long it takes to change something customer-visible today
- remove tooling, process, or architecture choices that do not earn their drag
- bias toward boring systems that the current team can operate confidently

- `How Pieter Levels Makes $2.7MYear With 0 Employees.txt`
- `How to get rich as a solo programmer  Pieter Levels and Lex Fridman.txt`

## 10. Ruben Gamez: Chase High-Intent Demand And Then Trace The Best Customers Back To Source

Use when:
- the founder is deciding what content, SEO, or acquisition motion to invest in
- traffic exists, but the team does not know which topics or sources actually produce good customers

Default:
- optimize for high-intent demand and then do more of whatever already sends the best customers

What this means in practice:
- Small search volume can still be valuable if the query sits close to the buying moment.
- A source with lower traffic but much higher trial or revenue conversion deserves more attention than a bigger vanity channel.
- Strong content often starts by validating demand around the job, not by publishing generic opinions.
- Once a page, referrer, or keyword converts unusually well, treat it like a signal to expand in that direction.

Failure mode:
- pouring effort into high-traffic sources that never produce serious buyers

Fastest next move:
- pull the top sources ranked by trial-to-paid or revenue conversion
- choose one source or keyword cluster that is clearly overperforming
- create the next three assets specifically for that cluster

Sources:
- `Freemium, Content Marketing + Finding the Source of Your Best Customers with Ruben Gamez.txt`
- `Ruben Gamez on his Methodic Content Strategy That Grew Bidsketch.txt`

## 11. Jon Yongfook: Force A Build Rhythm That Protects Distribution Work

Use when:
- the founder keeps saying marketing will start after one more feature
- the user is a solo founder or tiny team struggling to split time between product and go-to-market

Default:
- impose a fixed cadence so distribution work gets protected calendar space

What this means in practice:
- The point is not literal calendar purity. The point is to stop product polish from crowding out distribution forever.
- For a small software company, marketing often means useful launches, tutorials, docs, outreach, and educational assets around what was just built.
- A rigid rhythm is often better than an aspirational one because it forces tradeoffs.

Failure mode:
- hiding from go-to-market work behind another "almost ready" sprint

Fastest next move:
- block the next two weeks into one build week and one marketing week
- decide what outputs count before the cycle starts

## Quick Defaults

- If the question is "Should I raise prices?" -> default toward testing a real increase and judge the result on customer quality, support load, and revenue per visitor, not conversion alone.
- If the question is "How should I talk about this technical advantage?" -> translate it into the buyer's fear or desired outcome before using the technical term.
- If the question is "Should I self-host, host it for customers, or both?" -> default toward charging for the easiest, safest, best-configured hosted experience if the product economics support it.
- If the question is "How do I manage a huge feature launch?" -> start with the tentpole narrative, then decompose the dependency tree and ship room-cleaning prerequisites first.
- If the question is "Can I run several products at once?" -> only if they strengthen one audience-and-worldview flywheel.
- If the question is "Should I write content?" -> prefer a high-intent asset or repeat-use free tool tied to buyer pain, then do more of the sources that send the best customers.
- If the question is "Should I share revenue or metrics publicly?" -> only if it proves something your actual buyer wants to believe.
- If the question is "Should I keep building or start marketing?" -> default to a fixed build-versus-marketing cadence instead of waiting to feel ready.
- If the question is "Should I build this SaaS?" -> separate whether you want to build it from whether you want to operate it every week.
