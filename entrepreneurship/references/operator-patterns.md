# Operator Patterns

Use this file when the user wants sharper, founder-tested guidance instead of generic startup principles.

## How To Use This File

1. Pick only the section that matches the actual decision.
2. Borrow defaults only when the product mechanics, buyer behavior, and cost structure line up.
3. Name the pattern when it helps explain the recommendation.
4. If the user wants transcript-level specificity or named founder examples, read `goldmine-playbooks.md` or `founder-casebook.md` after choosing the matching section here.
5. If the case does not match a known pattern, fall back to `decision-playbook.md` and recommend a smaller test.

## 1. Audience-First Discovery

Use when:
- the founder has niche access, domain credibility, or community proximity
- the question is "who should we build for?" or "how do we find the right problem?"

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Start with audience discovery before product ambition. Enter the community, observe repeated complaints, and learn the exact language people use.
- Community is stronger than follower count. Relationships and trust matter more than raw impressions.
- "Audience-first" means putting the audience's needs first, not simply building a large audience.
- If the founder does not yet have an audience, become embedded in the target community and build one from within.
- Get uncomfortably specific if the market has a tight peer network. A narrow niche sharpens product scope, messaging, referrals, and eventually sellability.
- The best niche often looks smaller than the founder is comfortable with right before it starts compounding.

Questions to ask:
- Which communities does the founder already belong to or credibly serve?
- What problems do members repeatedly complain about or ask recommendations for?
- Does the founder have earned trust there, or only aspiration?
- Can the niche be described in concrete filters such as role, context, geography, regulation, or workflow?

Strong default move:
- Narrow to one community with real access, define the audience in concrete filters, and interview or observe there before broadening.

## 2. Distribution And Launch

Use when:
- the founder is choosing channels or preparing a launch
- the product may benefit from social or built-in sharing

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Product Hunt is an amplification machine, not a discovery engine. It works better once the product already has social proof, users, and a monetization model.
- Launch where the actual audience already pays attention. A niche Twitter audience can outperform generic launch platforms for tech products.
- If the product is inherently shared, use launch to seed the flywheel, not just to create a one-day spike.
- For marketplaces, over-serve the hardest side first and make the wedge obvious.
- Trust is part of distribution. Founder presence, clean design, and end-to-end customer experience all affect conversion.
- If the product's output is public and obviously useful, design usage so it doubles as distribution.
- For developer-facing or builder-facing tools, direct outreach plus an early-access cohort often beats paid influencer campaigns.

Questions to ask:
- Where does this buyer already spend attention and trust recommendations?
- Is the launch validating PMF, seeding a loop, or simply amplifying an already-working product?
- If this is a marketplace, which side is hardest to attract and how is the product obviously favoring them?
- Does using the product create visible proof, examples, widgets, or assets that sell the next user?
- Can the founder team credibly look, sound, and work like the target user in public?

Strong default move:
- Launch into the audience you already have or can credibly reach, use early access or direct outreach to tighten the loop, then use broader amplification only after early proof.

## 3. Pricing, Packaging, And Freemium

Use when:
- the question is about pricing, free tier, trials, lifetime deals, or sales motion

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Most founders underprice. Low prices often attract lower-commitment customers with higher support demands.
- Higher pricing can improve customer quality, patience, respect toward support, and overall economics even if conversion drops.
- Raise prices carefully and prefer testing on new customers first if trust with the existing base matters.
- Freemium is usually a bad default for self-funded SaaS unless free users create more users, materially improve activation, or cost very little to support.
- If the product is viral by nature, a free plan can outperform a trial because it keeps the sharing loop alive.
- If free is useful, keep it narrow and purposeful: a free plan, template, or tool should create distribution, qualify demand, or demonstrate the workflow the paid product handles better.
- Lifetime deals can work as an early validation or advocacy tool, but only with tight cost awareness, rising prices over time, and a clear plan to retire them.
- Packaging should match how the customer wants to buy and consume value. For libraries, education, or accumulating resources, one-time or annual access can beat another monthly subscription.
- AppSumo-style deals work best when they buy validation, reviews, and advocacy without creating ugly marginal-cost or support economics.
- In technical SaaS, pricing and support are linked. If fast human support is part of the wedge, price high enough to fund it and present it as part of the offer.

Questions to ask:
- Does free usage create more distribution, more activation, or just more support cost?
- Does the free surface naturally hand the user toward the paid product, or does it stop at generic utility?
- Are low prices hiding a quality problem in the customer base?
- Does the entry path match the product's complexity and ACV?
- Does this price point fund the response time, onboarding help, and docs quality the buyer expects?
- If recurring costs exist, are packaging and price still sustainable under heavy use?
- Is the customer buying ongoing service, or access to a growing asset they want to dip into when needed?
- If considering an LTD, will the offer create reviews, referrals, or product-led distribution in addition to cash?

Strong default move:
- Prefer paid or trial-first unless there is a strong viral loop or unusually low marginal cost; if using free, make it a narrow distribution or activation surface with an obvious path to paid. For non-usage-heavy libraries or education products, test annual or one-time packaging before defaulting to monthly SaaS orthodoxy.

## 4. Content, SEO, And Transparency

Use when:
- the founder is choosing content strategy, SEO, transparency, or audience-building work

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Content should start from buyer pain, not from generic "we should blog" instincts.
- Content can validate demand before the product is fully built if it targets a real job buyers are already trying to solve.
- Write where the right customers already pay attention. Traffic from the wrong audience is not a win.
- Prioritize intent over volume. Small, bottom-of-funnel keywords and pages can beat broad high-volume topics if they attract buyers who are ready to act.
- Strong content compounds when it becomes a hub: an in-depth resource that can later be repurposed into multiple formats.
- Templates, tutorials, comparison pages, free tools, and docs often outperform generic thought leadership because they sit closer to the buying moment.
- Refresh winning posts for search intent after the initial distribution spike instead of treating publishing as one-and-done.
- When one source sends unusually good customers, trace it and do more of that instead of celebrating blended traffic.
- Transparency works when it proves the thing the customer wants to believe.
- Free tools work best when they solve a repeat job, attract searchers with real intent, and naturally showcase or route into the paid product.
- Documentation can be both support deflection and acquisition if the product is technical and the docs are unusually useful.

Questions to ask:
- What exact customer problem should the next content asset help solve?
- Which blogs, creators, keywords, or communities already influence this buyer?
- Are we chasing raw traffic or buyer intent?
- Which sources already send the highest-converting trials, demos, or retained customers?
- If this is a free tool, does it solve a repeat problem that maps cleanly to the core product?
- Is transparency demonstrating product credibility to the buyer, or merely entertaining peers?

Strong default move:
- Build one buyer-relevant, high-intent asset such as a template hub, tutorial, comparison page, free utility, or deep guide, then instrument where the best customers come from and double down there.

## 5. Simplicity, Support, And Scope Control

Use when:
- support is rising
- the product feels bloated
- the founder is deciding whether to add complexity

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Do not use documentation to excuse a confusing product. A usable product beats great docs for an unusable product.
- Simplicity is not aesthetic minimalism. It reduces support burden, diligence burden, and founder stress.
- In technical products, fast competent support can be part of the value proposition, not just a cost center.
- Every new feature should earn its place through retained revenue, stronger activation, or a real distribution advantage.
- Complexity compounds operationally. The more moving parts the business has, the more painful diligence and handoff become.
- If support is a real differentiator for technical buyers, invest in it deliberately and charge accordingly instead of treating it as accidental founder labor.

Questions to ask:
- Is this request solving a core customer blockage or just adding edge-case breadth?
- Are docs fixing a true product gap or hiding an avoidable UX problem?
- Would best customers care if this feature disappeared?

Strong default move:
- Fix confusing UX and support-heavy friction before adding more surface area.

## 6. Hiring, Delegation, And Founder Focus

Use when:
- the founder is overloaded
- the business is growing but still founder-heavy
- the question is who to hire first

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Founders should do the job long enough to understand what good looks like before delegating it.
- In bootstrapped SaaS, the first leverage hire is often support or operations, not marketing.
- Hire when it hurts and when the founder can define the role well enough to spot a good operator.
- Mis-hires often happen because the founder delegates a function they never truly understood.
- If support volume is eating founder focus, that is a sign to document and hire earlier, not a badge of honor.

Questions to ask:
- Which tasks are signal-rich and founder-critical, and which are repetitive?
- Does the founder know enough about the role to hire well?
- What would founder attention unlock if support or ops were handled competently?

Strong default move:
- Keep the founder close to customers, but move repetitive support and ops off the founder once the pattern is understood.

## 7. Sellability And Founder-Light Operations

Use when:
- the user asks about exits, founder dependence, diligence, or optionality

Founder-tested heuristics:
- A good business is usually a sellable business. Build for operational quality first, not exit theater.
- Small, simple, profitable products can sell quickly because buyers understand them and diligence is lighter.
- Buyer quality and valuation improve when the business is profitable or has a credible path to profitability.
- Complexity, platform risk, and key-person risk all widen diligence and narrow the buyer pool.
- The founder should ask what breaks if they disappear for 30 days. Whatever breaks is the real founder dependence.
- Automation and documentation are dual-purpose: they make the business easier to run now and easier to diligence later.

Questions to ask:
- What revenue, trust, or product knowledge still lives only in the founder's head?
- Are the numbers clean, current, and easy to verify?
- Could the team run support, product, and reporting without daily founder intervention?

Strong default move:
- Reduce dependence, clean up numbers, and document operations before actively optimizing for an exit.

## 8. AI-Native And Fast-Moving Markets

Use when:
- the product is AI-native
- the market is moving fast enough that learning speed matters more than grand planning

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Use content and community work to accelerate learning if the founder is still forming deep market conviction.
- Solo founders should force an explicit build-versus-marketing cadence. A fixed rhythm beats vague promises to "market later."
- If an idea keeps living only in a notes app, that is often a sign of weak founder energy or weak urgency.
- Early access and builder programs create better feedback loops than paid influencer campaigns for developer-facing tools.
- Public shipping works best when the team looks and sounds like the user and can talk credibly about the workflow.
- Undifferentiated AI wrappers are fragile businesses. If anyone can ship the feature, the moat becomes distribution, trust, or workflow fit.
- Be careful with all-you-can-eat pricing when costs scale with usage.
- Use AI aggressively for internal leverage, but keep human taste on customer-facing communication when voice, curation, or trust matter.
- Keep human support and human-written communication where that is part of the trust wedge.

Questions to ask:
- Is the founder using distribution work to learn faster, or avoiding a product decision?
- Does the early-access group look like the future core user?
- What is the moat beyond shipping speed: owned distribution, product depth, trust, or workflow lock-in?
- Are variable costs aligned with packaging?

Strong default move:
- Set a fixed product-versus-marketing cadence, open a small builder cohort, ship visibly, and use it to learn before broad launch or expensive GTM bets.

## 9. Learning Loops And Intentional Friction

Use when:
- the team has traffic but still does not understand activation, onboarding, or segment quality
- the question is whether to add friction temporarily in order to learn faster

Founder-tested heuristics:
- Sometimes the highest-leverage move is to slow down a slice of the funnel to understand it.
- Segment the friction. Route only the users you most need to learn from into surveys, calls, or higher-touch onboarding.
- Treat lost conversion as research spend only when the question is specific and the learning window is short.
- High-touch is a diagnostic mode, not a permanent excuse for a weak product.

Questions to ask:
- What exact unknown are we trying to resolve?
- Which segment is most valuable to learn from right now?
- How quickly will we turn the learning back into product or messaging changes?
- Is traffic volume high enough that we can afford the temporary friction?

Strong default move:
- Run a short, segmented high-touch onboarding experiment aimed at one clear question, then remove the friction once the answer is clear.
