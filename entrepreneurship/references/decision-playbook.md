# Decision Playbook

Use this file for real startup and SaaS decisions, especially when the user is deciding where to spend product, GTM, pricing, or founder attention.

Before using this playbook, pull the matching checklist from `problem-map.md` so the answer is grounded in the right context.

## 1. Rebuild the Current State

Capture or infer:
- stage and urgency
- product and core user problem
- buyer, user, and current segment fit
- traction: revenue, retention, pipeline, activation, or customer pull
- current bottleneck
- constraints: runway, team, technical debt, credibility, channel access
- decision deadline

If the answer depends on missing facts, state the missing facts, make a working assumption, and continue.

## 2. Frame the Decision

Write a short internal frame:

- Decision: what is being chosen?
- Why now: what changed, broke, or became urgent?
- Objective: what outcome matters most in this window?
- Options: what are the real alternatives, including doing nothing?
- Constraint: what resource or risk is binding?
- Review point: when will the result be evaluated?

If the user presents a vague problem, reduce it to one decision first.

## 3. Collect Decision-Specific Evidence

Default evidence sources:

- cohorts, conversion, and retention
- win-loss and churn reasons
- support and onboarding friction
- pricing and plan behavior
- attribution and channel quality
- direct customer conversations

Prefer:

- retained behavior over expressed preference
- best-customer evidence over average-user noise
- specific channel proof over generic GTM advice
- repeated patterns over memorable anecdotes

## 4. Generate a Real Option Set

Include:
- the default or status quo
- the most focused credible option
- one stronger or more aggressive option if it is genuinely plausible
- a hybrid only when the hybrid does not hide indecision

Reject fake options that differ only in wording.

## 5. Score the Options

Score each option qualitatively across these lenses:

- customer pain: does it serve a painful, urgent problem?
- evidence: what real signal supports it today?
- distribution: can this team reliably reach the buyer?
- leverage: does it improve retention, revenue, or learning meaningfully?
- speed: how fast will it produce a trustworthy signal?
- reversibility: how expensive is it to unwind?
- focus cost: what does it distract from?
- operational burden: can the team actually support it?
- downside: what is the failure mode if this bet is wrong?

In early-stage SaaS, strong learning velocity often beats elegant long-range plans.

## 6. Apply Stage-Specific Heuristics

- Pre-PMF: optimize for painful problem clarity, direct customer signal, and fast learning.
- Early PMF: optimize for repeatability, onboarding, retention, and tighter ICP focus.
- Growth: optimize for channel scaling, economics, segmentation, and operational reliability.

Do not use growth-stage logic on a pre-PMF company.

## 7. Watch for Common Red Flags

Treat these as dominant warnings:

- trying to solve weak retention with more top-of-funnel
- adding features when segment focus is still fuzzy
- changing pricing before value is clear or repeat behavior exists
- using low pricing or freemium without a built-in viral loop or strong support economics
- choosing a GTM motion heavier than the price point can support
- choosing a channel the team cannot sustain or does not already understand
- spreading founder attention across too many partially committed bets
- relying on channels the team does not understand or cannot access repeatedly
- adding complexity that creates long-term support burden for a small team
- mistaking polite interest for buying intent

## 8. Prefer a Small Decisive Bet

When uncertainty is high, choose the next move that:
- tests the hardest assumption
- is cheap relative to runway and focus
- produces a clear signal
- does not require several unrelated things to go right at once

Good next moves are usually narrower than the founder first wants.

## 9. Return a Recommendation in This Shape

- Recommendation: choose X.
- Why now: explain why X beats Y and Z in this stage and context.
- Assumptions: list the facts that would change the answer.
- Risks: name the main downside and how to watch it.
- Metrics: say what to measure and over what window.
- Next step: name the exact action to take first.
- Missing context: if needed, list the top facts to collect next and how to get them fastest.

## 10. Default Decision Heuristics

Use these when the evidence supports them:

- Narrow the ICP before broadening the product.
- Fix activation and retention before paying to scale acquisition.
- Choose the channel that is repeatable, not the one that feels clever.
- Prefer direct conversations and usage evidence over abstract market theory.
- Raise complexity only after a simpler motion has clearly broken.
- Treat pricing as a quality and support filter, not just a monetization dial.
- Prefer the existing audience, community, or distribution edge when one exists.
- Protect founder focus as if it were runway, because it is.
