# Stair-Step Into Complexity

Use when:
- an early founder is choosing a first or next business model
- the founder is tempted to jump into a much harder market, channel, or company shape
- the goal is to build toward something bigger without taking a blind leap

Decision types:
- business model selection
- ambition shaping
- career sequencing
- idea selection

What must be true:
- the current step can teach a real skill, buyer truth, or distribution advantage for the next one
- the next move is adjacent enough that learning compounds

Why it works:
- each rung can buy credibility, cash flow, audience, and operational judgment that reduce the odds of the next bet

What to copy:
- pick the next business one notch beyond current skill or distribution
- use smaller offers, services, content, or simple products to learn the market before heavier software or team complexity
- keep experiments in the same arena long enough for learning to accumulate
- move up a rung when the current one has produced something transferable

What not to copy:
- jumping into the hardest model because it looks prestigious
- random pivots across unrelated markets
- staying forever on a low-learning rung out of comfort

Failure modes:
- no cumulative learning between attempts
- confusing busyness with progression
- using stair-steps as permission to avoid real customer risk

What to measure:
- transferable audience or channel access
- repeated customer pains learned on the current rung
- cash flow or leads that can finance the next move
- speed and cost of launching the next adjacent bet
